Use the calculator below to estimate approximate discounted cash flow (DCF) valuations of stocks of your choice. Before you work on the calculator, read this post I did on how to value stocks using DCF to understand the basics of sensible DCF usage, and how to avoid its misuse. And after you work on the calculator and arrive at DCF values for your chosen stocks, remember that your results will be proven wrong in the future (all valuations are wrong, you see!). :-) A tool like calculator or excel must only be used for approximate estimations, and never to seek perfections. What matters more is whether the business is simple to understand and whether you understand it or not. So, please focus on decisions, and not on outcomes of these tools. Now, try out this DCF Calculator (please use only absolute values, i.e., without decimals and percentage signs)...
DCF Calculator :


Help! Normalize.
Take 3 years' average free cash flow (FCF) as this starting number. FCF can be calculated from the Cash Flow Statement. FCF formula = Net Cash from/(used in) Operating Activities minus Purchase of Fixed Assets.


Help! Be conservative!
Be reasonable with your growth rate assumptions. Higher you set the growth rate, higher you set up the downside potential. Tip - For best businesses, cap the growth for first five years to 20% and 15% for next five.



Help! Discount rate - Rate at which you discount the future cash flows to the present value. It's your expected rate of return from the stock. 10-12% is good for stable, predictable businesses. 15% is good for relatively less predictable businesses. Using a high rate to discount the future cash just means you are willing to pay less today for the future cash and vice versa. It won't really matter what rate you use as long as you are being intellectually honest and conservative about future cash flows.


Help! Since it isn’t practical to forecast cash flows for an infinite number of years, it’s usual to end the DCF with a terminal value. The best terminal growth rate is 0%, and the highest you should go (for safest businesses) is 2%.



Help! Net Debt - Total debt of latest period minus Total cash and equivalents. Enter a negative value for cash positive companies.


Help! Margin of Safety (%). It's a good idea to have 30-40% margin of safety to allow for any errors in assumption.
